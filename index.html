02 / Featured project

Making beverage replenishment faster and more reliable.

An eight-month inventory and replenishment optimisation project for G.A.S Petrol Station Convenience Store in Waitoki, New Zealand.

Challenge

Busy staff had to serve customers, support fuel sales and prepare food while also restocking beverages. Products could remain in the backroom, causing shelf stockouts, lost sales and poor customer experience.

My role

As System Coordinator, I was responsible for inventory review, fast-moving SKU identification, storage analysis, layout and location-coding design, the replenishment SOP, checklist, trigger-line system and storage map.

Approach

Working with the project team, I helped turn sales and stock evidence into a practical visual system: Top 20 products, zoning, reusable coding templates, labels and short staff training during quiet trading periods.

Planned impact

Within the NZD 15,000 project plan, the pilot aimed for at least 30% fewer beverage stockouts, 25% faster backroom locating time, 90% replenishment accuracy for priority items and 90% SOP compliance.

Academic outcome: our group produced an integrated Project Management Report that linked the WBS, RACI chart, schedule, budget, risk register, change-control process, stakeholder communication plan and closure criteria. Project-management learning: a system succeeds only when it is simple enough for busy staff to use consistently. The plan kept the scope limited to the beverage category.

04 / Reflective learning

Learning I will carry into practice.

01 Turning a business problem into a defined project

In ICL Project Management, I learnt that a project should begin with a clearly defined problem rather than a preferred solution. In our G.A.S Waitoki project, the issue was not simply “improve stock”. Staff had to serve customers, support fuel sales, prepare food and replenish beverages at the same time. As a result, items could be available in the backroom but missing from the shelf. Defining the problem in this practical way helped our group connect stockouts with lost sales, customer experience and staff workload.

This learning was useful because I have often worked in fast-moving commercial roles where it is tempting to move directly to action. The course showed me that a project needs a scope statement, objectives, boundaries and stakeholders before solutions are designed. Our beverage-only scope was especially important: adding fuel, hot food, tobacco or new inventory software would have made the project too large for its time and budget. I learnt that a clear boundary protects the value of a project as much as a clear ambition does.

I applied this learning when contributing to the project report by linking the problem statement to the Work Breakdown Structure and later deliverables. In future, I will begin a workplace improvement initiative with a short problem definition: what is happening, who is affected, what evidence is available, what is included and what is excluded. I will also check this definition with stakeholders before investing effort. This habit will help me make better commercial decisions and prevent a promising idea from becoming an uncontrolled piece of work.

02 Using a WBS to turn scope into action

A specific skill I developed in ICL Project Management was creating and using a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS). Before this course, I understood complex work mainly through experience, meetings and urgent priorities. The WBS taught me to divide a large objective into visible work packages, deliverables and stages. For the beverage project, we moved from initiation and planning through design, development, training, implementation and evaluation. This made it possible to see that the storage map, coding system, SOP, labels and training were connected rather than separate tasks.

This was useful in both academic work and professional thinking. A WBS gave our group a common language for discussing what had to be delivered and what had to happen first. It also supported the RACI chart, schedule, budget and progress reporting in the report. I particularly learnt that a project plan is stronger when one structure supports several management decisions. For example, the WBS helped show why staff could not be trained until the layout, coding system and SOP were ready.

As System Coordinator, I applied this approach to the system-related work packages: fast-moving SKU identification, storage analysis, layout and coding design, trigger lines and the storage map. In future, I will use a simple WBS whenever I manage a campaign, process improvement or team initiative. I will review it with the people doing the work, make ownership clear and update it when approved changes occur. This will help me avoid missed tasks, duplicated work and unrealistic expectations while keeping practical business activity focused on outcomes.

03 Clarifying responsibility through RACI

Another important learning from ICL Project Management was the use of a RACI chart to clarify responsibility. RACI distinguishes who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted and Informed. I had used informal delegation in previous leadership roles, but the course showed me that a written responsibility matrix is especially valuable when work crosses different functional roles. It prevents a group from assuming that someone else will complete an important task, and it makes accountability visible before a deadline is missed.

In our project report, my System Coordinator role was responsible for several connected activities, including inventory review, fast-moving product identification, storage analysis, layout and coding design, SOP development, trigger-line design and replenishment-accuracy monitoring. The RACI chart also showed where I needed to work with the Operations Coordinator, Business Analyst and Project Manager. This was useful because it distinguished my responsibilities from the final accountability held by other roles. I learnt that collaboration is not weakened by clear ownership; it is made more effective.

I will apply this learning in future group assessments and workplace initiatives. At the start, I will agree the required outputs, one accountable person, the people responsible for delivery and the people who need consultation or information. I will then use the matrix as a conversation tool rather than a rigid document. This will support better delegation and coaching because team members will understand both their authority and the support available to them. It will also help me develop from a leader who solves every problem personally into one who creates capable, accountable teams.

04 Scheduling work around operational reality

ICL Project Management strengthened my understanding of schedule logic, critical paths and resource levelling. I learnt that a schedule is more than a list of dates. Activities must be placed in an order that reflects their dependencies, and critical activities need particular attention because a delay can affect the final completion date. In our project, analysis had to be completed before the layout was designed; the layout and SOP had to be ready before training; and staff training had to happen before the new replenishment process could be piloted.

This learning was useful because the project was designed for a busy convenience store, where staff time is limited and customer service cannot be interrupted. Our report used an eight-month schedule, with physical rearrangement and short training activities planned for quiet trading periods. I learnt that a technically sound solution may still fail if it ignores when people are actually available to implement it. Resource levelling made the plan more realistic by splitting work into smaller tasks and reducing overlap for the System Coordinator and Operations Coordinator.

I will apply this learning to future business activity such as trade-show preparation, campaign launches and process changes. I will identify the sequence of tasks, the non-negotiable milestones, the resources that may be overcommitted and the work that can be moved without harming the outcome. I will also make time for preparation and follow-up rather than concentrating only on the event or launch date. This will help me protect deadlines while respecting staff workload, customer needs and the normal operation of the business.

05 Monitoring, risk and stakeholder communication

A further learning from ICL Project Management was that monitoring is not only a reporting requirement; it is a way to identify problems early and respond before value is lost. Through the beverage project, I learnt to connect schedule, cost, quality, risk and stakeholder communication. The report used Earned Value Management and a traffic-light dashboard to show an understandable picture of project health. For example, a slightly delayed label-installation activity could be connected to staff availability, cost pressure and a practical recovery action.

This learning was useful because business leaders often receive too much information but not enough direction. The dashboard approach showed me how to communicate the key issue, its status, the reason for concern and the action required. Risk management also became more practical to me. Rather than listing generic risks, our group identified issues such as inconsistent use across shifts, limited backroom space, disruption during peak periods and weak reorder-point assumptions. Each risk had an owner, trigger, response and contingency.

I will apply this learning by using short, regular updates for future projects: progress against plan, emerging risks, decisions required and next actions. I will adapt the level of detail for the audience, whether that is a sponsor, a frontline team member, supplier or senior manager. In my future New Zealand leadership role, I will combine clear written communication with active listening, especially when a change affects daily work. This should build trust and make it easier for people to raise concerns before they become larger operational problems.

Peer feedback Learning from group Project Management feedback

Feedback from peers during the Project Management group report reinforced that I contributed practical business thinking, stayed committed to the group task and brought useful attention to how a proposed system would work in a busy store. The feedback also highlighted a development opportunity: I can involve others earlier when shaping an idea and make my recommendations more concise, with a clearer link between evidence, analysis and the proposed action. I found this balanced feedback valuable because it recognised a contribution I can build on while identifying a specific behaviour I can improve.

My reflection is that my commercial background can make me action-oriented. When I see an operational problem, I naturally want to move toward a workable solution. This can help a group keep momentum, but it can also mean that other members have less time to contribute their analysis or challenge an initial approach. In the beverage project, the best decisions required the combined perspectives of the Business Analyst, Operations Coordinator, Finance Analyst, Project Manager and System Coordinator. The RACI chart and weekly review process reminded me that a sustainable solution is not created by one person working quickly.

As a result, I will make three changes in future group work. First, I will agree roles, milestones and communication methods at the start. Second, I will ask for peer input before preparing a final recommendation. Third, I will use a simple structure in my writing: evidence, analysis, recommendation and action. I will continue requesting feedback after presentations and group assessments so that I can measure whether my communication is becoming clearer, more inclusive and more evidence-based.
